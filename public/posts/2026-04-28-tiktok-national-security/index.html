Why TikTok is a national security issue, not a free speech debate

The conversation about TikTok has been deliberately framed wrong. This isn't about what Americans can say. It's about who owns the infrastructure of their attention.

Kayla Victoria Bennett
Georgetown · Conservative voice
April 28, 2026 · 5 min read · Politics
KVB

Every time Congress holds a hearing on TikTok, the same argument surfaces: this is a free speech issue. Senators who can't explain what an algorithm is lecture tech executives about the First Amendment. Activists who have spent years demanding regulation of American platforms suddenly discover libertarian principles when the platform in question is Chinese-owned. The confusion is not accidental.

The conversation about TikTok has been deliberately framed wrong. And that reframing is itself a kind of victory for the people who benefit from the confusion.

What this debate is actually about

TikTok is not a free speech question. The First Amendment protects Americans from government censorship. It says nothing about who is permitted to own the infrastructure through which Americans communicate, what data that infrastructure may collect, or whose servers that data lives on.

The relevant question is simpler and more uncomfortable: Should the United States permit a company ultimately accountable to the Chinese Communist Party to operate one of the primary information environments for 170 million Americans?

That is not a question about speech. It is a question about infrastructure.

When you post on TikTok, you are not posting in a public square. You are posting inside a privately owned room, built with Chinese capital, governed by Chinese law, on servers that can be accessed by Chinese authorities under the National Intelligence Law of 2017 — a law that requires any Chinese company to cooperate with state intelligence work on demand.

The content you post may be protected speech. The room itself is not neutral territory.

The algorithm is the product

Most coverage of TikTok focuses on data collection: your location, your browsing habits, your biometric data. That is a legitimate concern. But it misses the deeper issue.

The more consequential tool is not what TikTok takes from you. It is what TikTok shows you.

The algorithm that determines which content reaches which users — which ideas spread, which candidates go viral, which narratives gain traction — is a political instrument. Every recommendation system makes choices. TikTok's choices are made by a company that operates under the authority of a government with explicit strategic interest in the domestic politics of its adversaries.

This is not speculation. China's doctrine of "discourse power" — the capacity to shape how populations understand the world — is a documented national priority. The idea that the world's most sophisticated short-video algorithm, operated by a Chinese company, subject to Chinese law, is simply neutral entertainment infrastructure strains credulity.

Linine will tell you that I am describing a threat that has never been demonstrated with hard evidence. She is technically correct. She is also missing the point entirely.

The infrastructure argument

We do not wait for a bridge to collapse before we inspect it. We do not wait for a power grid to fail before we audit its vulnerabilities. The standard for critical infrastructure is not proof of harm — it is unacceptable risk given the stakes.

In 2024, American intelligence officials confirmed that Chinese state actors had penetrated the networks of multiple major U.S. telecommunications companies — not to steal communications, but to map the architecture. To understand the system before they needed to use it.

The lesson is not that the harm occurred. The lesson is that the preparation occurred, quietly, for years, before anyone noticed.

TikTok represents the same category of risk — not because we have proof it has been used as a weapon, but because the conditions for its use as a weapon are fully in place, and the cost of being wrong is catastrophic.

The "American companies do it too" argument

They do. Facebook's data practices are indefensible. Google's surveillance infrastructure is extraordinary in scope. These are real problems that deserve serious regulatory attention.

But the equivalence argument proves too much. The fact that American companies collect data on American citizens does not mean that Chinese companies should also collect data on American citizens. Two surveillance problems do not cancel each other out.

More importantly: Facebook and Google are subject to American law. Their executives can be subpoenaed. Their servers can be seized. Their algorithms can be audited by American courts and regulators. They answer to a legal system whose interests, however imperfectly, are aligned with those of American citizens.

ByteDance answers to Beijing. That is a categorical difference, not a difference of degree.

What I actually believe

I am not asking for TikTok to be banned. I am asking for Americans to be honest about what the debate is about.

If Congress passes legislation requiring ByteDance to divest TikTok to an American owner — an owner subject to American law, American courts, and American oversight — then TikTok can continue operating. The content, the creators, the audience: all preserved. Only the ownership changes.

That is not censorship. It is the same standard we apply to airlines, banks, telecommunications companies, and every other sector where foreign ownership of critical infrastructure is restricted or prohibited.

The argument that divestiture is somehow an attack on free expression is a deliberate confusion of the issue — one that benefits a foreign government far more than it benefits American creators.
